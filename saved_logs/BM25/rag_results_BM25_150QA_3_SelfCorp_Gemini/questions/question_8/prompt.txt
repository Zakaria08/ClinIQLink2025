Document 1 (Title: Embryology and Manifestations of Diaphragmatic Hernia): Congenital diaphragmatic hernia (CDH) occurs in approximately 1 in 2,500-3,500 live births and represents a significant cause of respiratory distress in the neonatal period. The embryological basis involves defective formation of the diaphragm during weeks 4-12 of gestation, with several potential mechanisms depending on the specific anatomical variant. In most cases (80-85%), a posterolateral defect (Bochdalek hernia) results from incomplete fusion of the pleuroperitoneal membrane, which normally separates the pleural and peritoneal cavities during embryological development. This defect permits abdominal viscera to herniate into the thoracic cavity during a critical period of lung development. Left-sided defects predominate (80-85%) over right-sided ones (15-20%) due to earlier closure of the right pleuroperitoneal canal and the protective presence of the liver on the right side. The embryological timing of the diaphragmatic defect directly correlates with the severity of pulmonary hypoplasia, as herniated abdominal contents compete for thoracic space during crucial stages of lung development. Ultrasound diagnosis typically identifies the gastric fundus within the thoracic cavity as a pathognomonic finding in left-sided CDH, alongside mediastinal shift away from the affected side. This contrasts with other embryological anomalies such as annular pancreas, where pancreatic tissue encircles the duodenum causing intestinal obstruction, or hypertrophic pyloric stenosis, which develops postnatally rather than being a true embryological defect. Herniation through the inguinal canal represents a distinct pathophysiological process unrelated to diaphragmatic development.
Document 2 (Title: Differential Diagnosis of Infantile Vomiting Disorders): Non-bilious vomiting in the neonatal and early infancy periods represents a diagnostic challenge, with potential etiologies ranging from relatively benign conditions to life-threatening surgical emergencies. When presenting in the 2-8 week age range, infantile hypertrophic pyloric stenosis (IHPS) emerges as a principal consideration. This acquired condition involves progressive hypertrophy and hyperplasia of the circular muscle layer of the pylorus, resulting in gastric outlet obstruction. The pathophysiology involves abnormal innervation of the pylorus with deficient inhibitory neurons containing nitric oxide synthase, leading to uncoordinated contractions and subsequent muscular hypertrophy. This differs fundamentally from congenital anomalies present at birth, such as duodenal atresia, which typically manifests with bilious vomiting within the first 24-48 hours of life due to obstruction distal to the ampulla of Vater. Similarly, annular pancreas, where pancreatic tissue encircles the duodenum, presents with early obstruction and bilious emesis. Gastroschisis and other midline defects resulting from failed ventral wall fusion present with visible abdominal wall defects rather than isolated vomiting. The classic presentation of pyloric stenosis includes progressive, projectile non-bilious vomiting, visible peristaltic waves, and, on careful examination, a palpable 'olive' in the right upper quadrant representing the hypertrophied pylorus. Ultrasound confirmation demonstrates increased pyloric muscle thickness (>3mm) and elongated pyloric channel (>14mm).

Amitriptyline is an FDA-approved medication to treat depression in adults. Amitriptyline is in the tricyclic antidepressant (TCA) drug classification and acts by blocking the reuptake of both serotonin and norepinephrine neurotransmitters. The three-ring central structure and a side chain are the basic structure of tricyclic antidepressants. Amitriptyline is a tertiary amine with strong binding affinities for alpha-adrenergic, histamine (H1), and muscarinic (M1) receptors.[6] Amitriptyline increases noradrenergic or serotonergic neurotransmission by blocking the norepinephrine or serotonin transporter (NET or SERT) at presynaptic terminals. Chronic treatment with amitriptyline desensitizes presynaptic autoreceptors and heteroreceptors, producing long-lasting changes in monoaminergic neurotransmission.[7] Amitryptiline is more sedating and has increased anticholinergic properties than other TCAs. As with other antidepressants, the onset of therapeutic action typically begins at approximately 2 to 4 weeks. Amitriptyline toxicity can be serious and even fatal. In treating the toxicity, the top priority is stabilizing the patient, which may require admission to the ICU for monitoring. The most important steps include - protecting the airways, breathing, and stabilizing circulation. Some patients may need tracheal intubation; if required, administer supplemental oxygen. All patients suspected of tricyclic antidepressant overdose should receive gastrointestinal decontamination. This should include large-volume gastric lavage followed by activated charcoal.[41] Seizures secondary to overdose are treatable with diazepam or lorazepam. If the patient is hypotensive, an IV bolus of isotonic crystalloid is a therapeutic option. Vasopressors are the next choice if the patient remains hypotensive despite fluid resuscitation. If QRS exceeds 100 msec, intravenous sodium bicarbonate is the appropriate intervention. Sodium bicarbonate is cardioprotective (it increases extracellular sodium concentration) and diminishes the effect of amitriptyline on the cardiac membrane, resulting in less sodium channel blockage. Alkalization favors the neutral form of amitriptyline and decreases the amount of active cyclic antidepressants.
